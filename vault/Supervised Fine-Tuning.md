# Supervised Fine-Tuning

> Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) is the second training stage where a pretrained base model is trained on curated (prompt, ideal-response) pairs to teach it to follow instructions rather than just predict the next token.

## The core idea

After [[Pretraining]], a base model is an internet-text completion engine — brilliant at pattern-matching but useless as an assistant. It has no concept of "answer my question." SFT changes the objective from "predict random web text" to "given this prompt, produce this desired response."

The mechanics are identical to pretraining: cross-entropy loss over next-token predictions, same backpropagation. The only difference is the data. Instead of trillions of tokens scraped from the web, you train on tens of thousands to millions of hand-curated conversations labeled by humans or generated by a stronger model and filtered.

Karpathy frames this as "swapping the dataset, not the algorithm." The gradient update nudges the model's weights so that helpful, coherent assistant-style responses become high probability. The pretraining knowledge stays intact — SFT reshapes *behavior*, not *knowledge*.

A key practical constraint: SFT data quality dominates quantity. One perfectly written conversation is worth thousands of noisy ones. Companies like OpenAI use contractor annotation pipelines; open efforts (Alpaca, ShareGPT) use GPT-4 to synthesize demonstrations.

## Why it matters / where it fits

SFT is the bridge between [[Base vs Instruct Models]] — it is literally the step that converts a base model into an instruct model. It also sets the behavioral baseline that [[RLHF and RL]] then refines. Without solid SFT, RLHF has nothing good to reinforce.
